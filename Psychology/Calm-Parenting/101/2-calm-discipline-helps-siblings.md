### Rethinking Time-outs ❌

Most parents with more than one child use timeouts when their children fight, to separate their kids and hopefully teach them to be nicer to each other. But they are punishment ❌ and they do not actually teach kids to be nicer to each other. In fact, they worsen kids behavior. 

Why Timeouts don't work:
1. **Creates shame** - Kids believe that if they were 'good', they would be able to stop the bad feeling that makes them behave badly. Shame creates a negative cycle, reinforces the feeling that they are a bad person.

2. **Don't help kids to learn emotional regulation**. you send him off to room by himself ➡️ he calms down. But he has not expressed those feelings ➡️ feelings pushed down ➡️ No conscious control ➡️ Kids Angrier, less emotional regulation. 

    You see him walk by his sister and push her for no reason.

3. **Does not solve the problem** between children that caused one to lash out. Parents think they have taken care of problem, they give a lecture. But child is not prepared to navigate conflict next time.

4. **weakens bond with child**: The bond is the only reason kids behave to begin with. You use timeouts, bond weakens ➡️ escalates misbehavior.

5. **Fuels Power struggle**: Kids feel powerless, they take it out on someone less powerful - *sibling*.

6. **Fear - Symbolic abandonment**: Love withdrawal, Sibling rivalry stems from child fear that he is losing your love to his sibling, any discipline involving love withdrawal worsens their rivalry.

There is no *right* way to do timeouts. No studies examined long term emotional health of children grew up with timeouts. We do have evidence that they don't work and potentially causing the recurrence of misbehavior.

### Consequences and limits

**Natural** *consequences* are result of child's behavior and you have **nothing** to do with creating them. For eg: If he throws water on brothers face, brothers may not want to play water fights with him again.

`Natural consequences teach child more effectively than any 'lessons' you could devise. Resist stepping in to 'rescue' child from natural consequence`

**Limits** are boundaries you establish about what behavior is permitted in your family. 

> If child crosses limits ➡️ lovingly ❤️ redirect him

> If he can't follow limit ➡️ remove him from situation. This is *NOT* punishment. It is holding that boundary

eg: If no spraying on face is a rule in water fights. It's your job to hold that limit.

- Remind him the rule with ❤️. Ask if he can stop or need help.
- If he says he will stop, he might because he consciously committing.

- Before he rejoins game. Ask him why is the rule and How will he remember. and what if he gets excited? plan it
- Tell him the natural consequences
- What if he forgets in 5 minutes. Tell him -

```
"It was just too hard for you today. we will try again tomorrow.. You're growing every day and will be able to control more yourself and be a responsible playmate."
```

He cries (better outcome). Grieving is a part of process. He will control better next time.

 ### What if empathy does not work

 You will be amazed how well empathy works. It is so effective, we will be taken by surprise when it does not work. Empathy is not a trick. It is means of connection and helping child process emotions.

 1. **Empathy makes my child cry harder** - It means it is working. When something bad happens and big feelings locked up and someone with whom you felt safe hugs you, you get in touch with feelings and burst into tears. It is the same. Good cry positively transforms our emotional state.

 2. **Empathy does not stop the tantrum** - Once he is in fight or flight, words don't help. Do not label the emotion, use fewer words just enough to know your compassion. Empathy just helps your child to show feelings, which starts healing.

 3. **Keep repeating "You're sad and frustrated. He gets mad and tells me not to say it"** - If you merely parrot, he sense that, irritates them. Let yourself feel how much he is torn, you will get tears in your eyes. And he will feel understood.
 
 > With Toddler - Acknowledging reassures. saying "You're so mad" works.
 
 > But with older kids - Telling them makes angrier. Like us, they don't want to be analyzed or manipulated. They want to know that we see their pov.

 Imagine if you are sad and your partner keeps repeating that you are sad. 
 
 *No need to label the emotion. Understand his perspective*

```
 I am sorry you woke up from your nap to find that daddy took your brother to shop without you... I know you wish you could have gone. 
```

 As kids grow - Oh sweetie, I'm sorry, it is so hard - will get your empathy across.

 * In middle of tantrum - words just escalate their anger. Only thing they need to know is they are sage and you are ready with a hug whenever they are ready.
 
4. **I empathize with the emotions, but then she is still upset about it** - Empathy many times defuses her emotions, But if she is reacting to a big problem in her life - we need to help her solve it. 

```
You are so mad at your brother. I think he needs to hear how you feel. Let's go find your brother, and I will stay with you while you tell him.
```

Crying is a good thing - They will build the resilience. They feel safe enough with you to let themselves feel their disappointment fully.

5. **I say "You are mad but we don't hit," and 10 mins, he hits again.** - When you use the word **but** kids don't feel that their feelings are really acknowledged. Use `And` 

***You can be as mad as you want AND we do not hit.***

🔑 Kids who hit have fear locked inside. They need to cry and shake and yawn and show you that fear. only then, hitting stops

Empathy always works to reconnect. If empathy is not working - *Stop* trying to come up with **right** words. Imagine yourself as the child in that situation, what do you wish your parent would do - Do that

With more than one child ➡️ disconncection happens ➡️ problems

Advantages:
- child feels treasured. Antidote to sibling rivalry.

- feels safe ➡️ he can regulate himself emotionally.
- gets help with emotions before ➡️ less crisis moments.
- enjoys more positive interactions ➡️ close siblings, less conflict - a postiive cycle.

1. **Use Routines**: Routine makes them feel safe. Does not mean to enforce rigid schedule. Notice what seems to work for all and find something you can all look forward to.

2. **Empathy**: when trying to move kids through schedule we often find their feelings inconvenient. 

💬 Child: I Hate chicken. Why do we have to eat this again?

*You may be tempted to lecture how hard you worked, how his body needs protein and why we should be grateful for healthy dinner. But acknowledge his pov*

📝 Parent: You don't feel like eating chicken tonight, huh? I guess we have had lot of chicken lately. And it is not your favorite. I know. I bet you wish you could have pasta everynight, don't you?

child may not happily eat chicken, but will be a calm dinner and some good problem solving about nutrition and how to balance preferences. Child will feel closer.

3. **Daily Rough and tumble**: Children build up anxiety, mild fear through out the day. Laughter, giggling is best medicine to reduce this stress buildup. Best way to make them laugh is physical rough and tumble that provokes mild fear response. 

> Everytime you laugh with someone, you are building trust. Bonding with parent and sibling. Schedule 15 mins belly laughter, peek-a-boo, chasing them, pillow fight. 

4. **Special time**: Leave this time completely unstructured. Kid is incharge. This is not just making biscuits together, reading time. Just resist the urge to take active role just describe what you see him doing and take on a role what he suggests. 

* Special time works wonders, Be ruthless - No Meetings, entertaining, work emails in eventing. Even if you give up pay for these hours it is worth it. Still if child can't manage her emotions - Time-in, Scheduled melt downs.
